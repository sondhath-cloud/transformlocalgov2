What is an Organizational "Operating System"?

Just like a computer's operating system manages hardware and software to run programs efficiently, your organization's "operating system" is the underlying framework that determines how your people, processes, and technology work together to serve your community.

Computer Operating System

Memory Management

Allocates RAM efficiently to run multiple programs simultaneously

Process Control

Manages CPU resources and task scheduling for optimal performance

Hardware Interface

Provides drivers and APIs for hardware components to communicate

File System

Organizes and manages data storage and retrieval

Security Layer

Protects against viruses, malware, and unauthorized access

Organizational Operating System

People Management

Allocates human resources efficiently across departments and projects

Workflow Control

Manages processes and procedures for optimal service delivery

Technology Interface

Integrates software systems and tools for seamless operations

Knowledge System

Organizes and manages information, policies, and procedures

Culture Layer

Protects against burnout, turnover, and disengagement

Change Management Tasks During System Selection

Identify and map stakeholders across all levels and departments who will be affected by the new system. Document their interests, influence levels, and potential concerns to create a stakeholder engagement matrix.

Form a change network by recruiting change champions and advocates from different departments and levels. These individuals will help communicate benefits, gather feedback, and support their peers through the transition.

Develop a communication strategy that outlines how you'll keep stakeholders informed throughout the selection and implementation process. Include communication channels, frequency, key messages, and target audiences for each phase.

Conduct initial stakeholder meetings to explain why the system change is happening, the selection process, and how people can provide input. This builds trust and reduces uncertainty early.

Create feedback mechanisms such as surveys, focus groups, or suggestion boxes so employees can voice concerns, ask questions, and contribute to requirements gathering during selection.

Perform an organizational readiness assessment to understand your organization's capacity for change. Evaluate past change initiatives, current change fatigue levels, and cultural factors that might affect adoption.

Conduct impact analysis to identify how different roles, processes, and departments will be affected by the new system. Map out what will change in daily work for various user groups.

Assess current state processes and document existing workflows, pain points, and workarounds. This creates a baseline for comparison and helps identify improvement opportunities.

Evaluate change capacity by reviewing other ongoing initiatives and organizational bandwidth. Determine if timing is appropriate or if adjustments are needed.

Develop the change vision and business case that clearly articulates why the change is necessary, what benefits it will bring, and how it aligns with organizational strategy. Make this compelling and relatable to different audiences.

Establish governance structures including a steering committee, project board, or change advisory board to oversee both technical and people aspects of the initiative.

Align leadership team by ensuring executives and senior leaders understand their role in sponsoring the change. Conduct sponsor assessments and provide coaching if needed.

Define success metrics for both the technical implementation and adoption. Include user adoption rates, proficiency levels, process improvements, and business outcome measures.

Create a change management plan that outlines your approach, activities, timelines, resources, and responsibilities for managing the people side of change throughout the project lifecycle.

Develop a resistance management strategy that anticipates potential sources of resistance and outlines proactive and reactive approaches to address concerns.

Plan the training approach at a high level, including training needs analysis methodology, delivery options, timing considerations, and resource requirements.

Design the organizational change management structure by determining team roles, responsibilities, and integration points with the project team.

Facilitate requirements workshops that include change management perspectives on user experience, adoption barriers, and process design. Ensure the human element influences system selection criteria.

Include change criteria in vendor evaluation such as system usability, learning curve, change management support from vendors, and implementation approach flexibility.

Advocate for user-centered design by ensuring end-user perspectives are represented in system demonstrations, evaluations, and decision-making processes.

Document change implications of different system options to help decision-makers understand people and process impacts of each choice.

Begin building awareness through town halls, newsletters, intranet updates, or team meetings about the upcoming change and the selection process.

Start cultural preparation by addressing any cultural barriers to change, promoting a growth mindset, and celebrating examples of successful adaptation.

Identify early adopters and influencers who can be engaged more deeply in the selection process and groomed to support broader rollout later.

Gather baseline data on current satisfaction, pain points, system usage patterns, and attitudes toward change that can be measured again post-implementation.

Develop a change risk register identifying people-related risks such as key person dependencies, potential resistance hotspots, competing priorities, or cultural barriers.

Create mitigation plans for identified risks with specific actions, owners, and triggers for implementation.

Plan for business continuity during transition periods, including how work will continue if adoption is slower than expected.

Determine change management resource needs including team members, budget, tools, and external support required throughout the initiative.

Build the change team by recruiting or assigning change managers, communication specialists, training developers, and other necessary roles.

Secure budget allocation for change management activities including communications, training, change champion programs, and celebration events.

Starting these activities during system selection ensures change management is embedded from the beginning rather than bolted on later, significantly improving the likelihood of successful adoption and realization of benefits.

Helpful Change Management Support

Announce the system selection through multiple channels to inform all stakeholders which system was chosen and why. Share the decision rationale, expected benefits, and high-level timeline to build understanding and buy-in.

Refine stakeholder analysis now that the specific system is known. Update your stakeholder map to reflect who will be most impacted by this particular solution and adjust engagement strategies accordingly.

Expand the change champion network by recruiting additional advocates who are enthusiastic about the selected system. Provide them with detailed information so they can speak knowledgeably with their peers.

Launch regular communication cadence including newsletters, intranet updates, team briefings, and town halls to maintain momentum and keep people informed as planning progresses.

Create communication materials such as FAQs, fact sheets, and presentation templates that explain the new system, its capabilities, and what it means for different user groups.

Establish two-way dialogue channels through office hours, Q and A sessions, online forums, or feedback surveys where employees can ask questions and express concerns in real time.

Conduct detailed impact analysis for the specific system selected. Map exactly how workflows, responsibilities, interfaces, integrations, and daily activities will change for each role and department.

Perform gap analysis comparing current state capabilities and processes with the future state enabled by the new system. Identify specific changes required in processes, skills, and behaviors.

Assess organizational change readiness more precisely now that people know what system is coming. Measure current attitudes, concerns, and readiness levels across different groups.

Identify high-impact groups who will experience the most significant changes and require additional support, training, or change interventions.

Document process changes by working with process owners to redesign workflows that will be affected by the new system. Create future state process maps and identify optimization opportunities.

Evaluate cultural fit between the selected system and organizational culture. Identify where cultural shifts or adaptations will be necessary for successful adoption.

Conduct executive sponsor coaching to prepare leaders for their visible role in championing the change. Provide talking points, presentation materials, and guidance on effectively communicating support.

Create a sponsor roadmap outlining specific activities leaders need to perform at each project phase, from kickoff through go-live and stabilization.

Align middle management who will be critical to supporting their teams through the transition. Brief managers on their role, expected changes, and how to address team concerns.

Develop leadership communication plan detailing when and how leaders should communicate about the change, including kickoff messages, milestone updates, and recognition of progress.

Address leadership concerns privately through one-on-one meetings to resolve any hesitations or misalignments before they become visible to the broader organization.

Finalize the comprehensive change management plan with specific activities, timelines, resource assignments, and dependencies aligned to the implementation schedule.

Develop the training strategy and plan including needs analysis for the specific system, curriculum design approach, delivery methods, trainer requirements, and scheduling considerations.

Create the communications plan with detailed message calendars, content themes, delivery channels, and responsibility assignments for each phase of implementation.

Design the reinforcement and sustainability plan outlining how you will support adoption after go-live through coaching, performance support, ongoing communications, and continuous improvement processes.

Build the resistance management approach with specific strategies for different sources and levels of resistance, including escalation paths and intervention techniques.

Develop the adoption measurement framework defining key performance indicators, data collection methods, reporting cadence, and targets for user adoption and proficiency.

Conduct training needs analysis for the specific system to identify what skills and knowledge different user groups need to develop. Consider varying proficiency levels and learning preferences.

Design training curriculum including course outlines, learning objectives, delivery formats, duration, and sequencing for different audiences from basic users to power users to administrators.

Identify training resources needed such as trainers, training environments, materials development tools, learning management systems, and scheduling resources.

Develop train-the-trainer program if using an internal trainer model. Create materials and schedule sessions to prepare trainers to deliver effective instruction.

Plan training logistics including scheduling approach, classroom or lab requirements, equipment needs, participant selection process, and attendance tracking methods.

Begin developing training materials such as instructor guides, participant workbooks, quick reference guides, and e-learning modules based on system functionality and business processes.

Create a training communication plan to inform people when training will be available, how to register, what to expect, and why participation is important.

Develop key messages and talking points for different audiences explaining what the system does, why it was chosen, how it will benefit them, and what changes to expect.

Create a change story or narrative that connects the new system to organizational vision and strategy in a compelling, memorable way.

Design visual identity for the change initiative including project name, logo, tagline, or theme that makes the initiative recognizable and builds positive association.

Produce awareness campaign materials such as posters, screensavers, video teasers, or countdown communications to build anticipation and maintain visibility.

Develop stakeholder-specific content tailored to different groups such as executives, managers, end users, customers, and external partners explaining relevant impacts and expectations.

Plan launch events or kickoff activities to formally introduce the implementation and generate enthusiasm. Consider town halls, departmental meetings, or celebration events.

Redesign impacted business processes to optimize workflows for the new system rather than simply replicating old processes. Document these changes and secure approvals.

Update policies and procedures that will be affected by the new system. Draft new documentation that reflects future state operations.

Revise roles and responsibilities where job duties will change significantly. Update position descriptions, performance expectations, and accountability structures as needed.

Plan organizational structure changes if the new system enables or requires different team configurations, reporting relationships, or resource allocations.

Prepare physical workspace modifications if the new system requires different equipment, layouts, or collaboration spaces.

Recruit and onboard change champions from all affected areas. Select individuals who are respected, enthusiastic, and have strong networks within their departments.

Train change champions on their role, the new system, change management principles, and how to support their colleagues. Provide them with toolkits and resources.

Establish change champion network structure including regular meetings, communication channels, reporting mechanisms, and recognition programs to keep them engaged and informed.

Create change champion toolkit with talking points, FAQs, demonstration access, feedback collection tools, and other resources they need to be effective advocates.

Define escalation processes so change champions know how to route questions they cannot answer or concerns that require leadership attention.

Update the change risk register with implementation-specific risks now that the system is known. Add risks related to technical complexity, resource constraints, or adoption challenges.

Develop detailed risk mitigation plans with specific actions, owners, timelines, and success measures for each significant risk.

Establish issue management process for tracking and resolving change-related concerns, resistance, or barriers that arise during implementation.

Create contingency plans for potential scenarios such as delayed timelines, resource shortages, or unexpected resistance that could derail adoption.

Plan for quick wins by identifying early opportunities to demonstrate value and build confidence in the new system.

Conduct readiness workshops with impacted teams to help them understand what is changing, prepare for the transition, and voice any concerns or questions.

Create readiness assessments or checklists that teams and individuals can use to evaluate their preparedness for the upcoming changes.

Develop change readiness reporting to track preparedness across the organization and identify areas needing additional support before go-live.

Begin building excitement through sneak peeks, demo sessions, or prototype walkthroughs that give people positive exposure to the new system.

Address concerns proactively by creating forums where people can discuss worries, ask difficult questions, and receive honest, thoughtful responses.

Integrate change management into project governance by securing representation on steering committees, project boards, and decision-making forums to ensure people considerations influence technical decisions.

Align change activities with project milestones to ensure communication, training, and engagement activities are synchronized with technical implementation phases.

Coordinate with technical teams on testing strategies, data migration communication, cutover planning, and support model design to ensure seamless user experience.

Establish integration points with other initiatives or changes happening concurrently to minimize change fatigue and identify synergies or conflicts.

Define success criteria jointly with project and business leaders that include both technical delivery and adoption metrics.

Design post-go-live support model including helpdesk preparation, super user programs, floor walking support, office hours, and escalation procedures.

Develop performance support tools such as job aids, quick reference guides, video tutorials, searchable knowledge bases, or in-application guidance.

Plan super user program by identifying candidates, defining their role, planning their training, and establishing how they will support colleagues.

Create support communication strategy so users know how to get help, where to find resources, and what to do when they encounter problems.

Establish feedback collection mechanisms for post-implementation to capture user experiences, identify issues quickly, and demonstrate responsiveness to concerns.

Define baseline metrics for current performance, satisfaction, efficiency, and other measures that will be compared post-implementation to demonstrate impact.

Develop adoption dashboards or reporting to track user login rates, feature utilization, transaction volumes, and other indicators of system usage and proficiency.

Create evaluation surveys and tools to assess user satisfaction, confidence levels, perceived benefits, and remaining barriers at various intervals post-implementation.

Plan evaluation schedule defining when assessments will occur, who will conduct them, how results will be analyzed, and how findings will drive improvement actions.

Establish continuous improvement process for ongoing refinement based on user feedback, adoption data, and emerging needs.

Finalize change management team by confirming all roles are filled, clarifying responsibilities, and ensuring team members have necessary skills and bandwidth.

Secure budget approvals for all planned change activities including training development, communication production, events, tools, and external support if needed.

Procure necessary tools and resources such as survey platforms, learning management systems, collaboration tools, or design services needed to execute the plan.

Establish vendor relationships if external support is needed for training development, communication design, coaching, or change management advisory services.

Confirm resource commitments from business units for subject matter experts, testers, trainers, change champions, and other roles that support implementation success.

Beginning these activities after system selection but before implementation starts ensures the organization is truly prepared for the change, people feel informed and engaged, and the foundation is laid for successful adoption when the system goes live.
